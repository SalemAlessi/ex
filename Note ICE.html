supercharging system

Definition

  1. The method of increasing the air capacity or density of an engine in known as supercharging.
  2. supercharger is merely a blower or a compressor that provides a denser charge to the engine.

objectives

  1. for ground installations it is used to produce a gain in the power output of the engine
  2. for aircraft installations , in addition to produce a gain in the power output at sea level , it also enables the engine to maintain a higher power output as altitude is increased

SI engines

  1. supercharging in SI engine is employed only in aircraft and racing car engines.
  2. Apart from increasing the volumetric effciency of the engine , supercharging results in an increase in the intake temperature of the engine.

CI engine

  1. in case of CI engines , supercharging dose not result in any combustion problem , rather it improves combustion.
  2. Increase Of Pressure And temperature of the induced air reduces ignition delay, and hence the rate of pressure rise results in a better, quieter and smoother combustion

supercharging methods

  1. mechanical supercharger
  2. Turbocharging

Fuel injection system

  1. the fuel injection system most occur at the correct moment.
  2. it should supply the fule in correct quantity as required bt the varying engine loads.
  3. the injected fuel must be broken into very fine droplets.
  4. the spray pattern should ensure rapid mixing of fuel and air.
  5. it should supply equal quantites of metered fuel to all the cylinders in a mulit cylinder engines.
  6. the beginning and the end of injection should be sharp.

element of fule injection system

  1. pumping element : to supply fuel from fuel tank to cylinder .
  2. metering element : to meter fule supply as per load and Speed .
  3. distribution element : to divide the metered fuel equaally among the cylinder
  4. timing control : to adjust the start and stop of injection.
  5. mixing element : to atomize and distrbute the fuel within the combustion chamber.

Lubrication system

    Purpose Of Lubrication

  1. to teduce the frictional resistance of the engine to a minimum to ensure maxmimum mechanical effciency.
  2. protect the engine against wear.
  3. serve as a cooling agent by picking uo heat.
  4. remove all impurities from the lubricated region.
  5. form a seal between piston rings and the cylinder walls to prevent blower.

Alternative fuel and combustion chambers in I.C.E

Conventional fuels

fossil fuels (petroleum) , coal , and nuclear materials such as uranium

Alternative fuels.

  1. also known as non-conventional fuels.
  2. any materials or substances that can be used as fuels , other than Conventional fuels.
  3. which is consumed to provide energy to power an engine .

Ethanol

  1. mainly produced by the sugar fermentation process
  2. can also be manufactured by the chemical process of reacting Ethanol with steam.
  3. A clear colorless liquid , its biodegradable , low in toxicity and causes little environmental pollution if spilt.
  4. Ethanol is a high octane fuel and has replaced lead as an octance enhancer in petrol.
  5. widely sold in the USA.

Biodiesel

  1. Biodiesel contains no petroleum, but it can be blended at any level with petroleum diesel.
  2. it can be used in compression-ignition (diesel) engine with little or no modifications.
  3. Biodiesel is a form of diesel fuel manufactured from : vegetable oils , animal fats , recycled restaurant greases.

Hydrogen

  1. A potenially emissions-free alternative fuel produced from domestic resources.
  2. not widely used today as a transportation fuel.
  3. not occur free in nature in usful quantities , but it is manufactured.

Compressed Natural Gas

  1. a fossil fuel substitute for gasoline (petrol) , diesel fuel and propane LPG.
  2. a more Environmentally alternative to Conventional fuels.
  3. it is much safer than fuels in the event of a spill , because natural gas is lighter than air and disperses quickly when relesed.
  4. made by compressing natural gas (wich is mainly composed of methane , CH4 ) to less than 1 percent of the volume it occupies at standard atmospheric pressure .
